Day Five: Masking Email Addresses

January 5, 2025

Now you have one or two new email addresses, you can accommodate a couple of different levels of security for your online activities -- excellent!  But what about all these million and one different activities? There's church choir, band practice, the kids rollerblading lessons, family newsletters, holiday shopping and groceries. Does this require a million email accounts?

No: you are not going to need one million email accounts to do this. You will use masking and aliases. Because fortunately an email account is not the same as an email address.  Let me explain.

All email accounts have an email address attached. But there are other ways of getting email sent to that account by addressing to a different address instead. If you've ever used automatic email forwarding, you've done this before: sent an email to one address only to have it forward to a different account's inbox. On Gmail, you may have used "subaddressing" by adding a "+" after your username, which is a nifty way to stay organized. Of course, your email address "myname" is still visible there in the address line!

Masks and Aliases keep your account's address unknown. These are alternative addresses that forward to your account behind the scenes. This is a great tool for thwarting detection in the personal data economy. With masking and aliases, you can give out many different addresses, while still receiving email to the same base account, making it more challenging to link those datasets together.

You can also really easily track where someone snagged your email address from. If you only give out one mask per company, and that company gets bought, hacked, or otherwise co-opted such that a new company starts sending messages to that dedicated address, you'll know exactly who gave you away. 

It's also a great tool for keeping your life organized. Tired of getting spam from a store you bought from thirteen years ago? Delete the mask and it can't reach you. Looking for every bill that came in from Verizon? Search for its email mask instead of through subject headers.

Ensure you have one or more masking/alias services. These three are great and even include a browser plugin so you can generate masks and aliases at will:

  • Protonmail comes with masking built in through their partner SimpleLogin, in France.
  • Firefox Relay works with your Mozilla account to make aliases to forward to an account of your choice; they also have a phone number masking service too.
  • DuckDuckGo offers "Email Protection" which gives you an @duck.com account, and automatically generated masks and aliases as well. When you get an email through DuckDuckGo's masking service, it removes all trackers, and allows you to respond with just the mask as your email address.

Once you've signed up for one of these, point them to your new general email address of choice.

Then add the relevant extension for Firefox (or whatever plugin for the browser you've chosen) to your browser.

And that's it!

You've done it! You have a sense of your different email needs from your roadmap. You have set up more than one account that can handle different levels of security. You have set up masking to offer an obfuscation service for these email accounts. Your email can be Balkanized moving forward, split into these different buckets for your different personalities, needs, activities, and threat models. Now you can pick and choose your email for the occasion. Bravo!

We are now ready to move forward, and move your existing email to a better place.
